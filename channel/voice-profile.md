## Universal Voice Rules

These rules apply to every topic — cults, missing persons, conspiracies, institutional corruption, internet crimes. None of these rules are topic-specific.

---

### Rule 1: Declarative Factual Claims — No Modal Qualifiers

**Definition:** State facts as facts. Use declarative sentences ("X happened", "X was Y") rather than hedged constructions ("X may have happened", "X seems to have been Y", "X was reportedly Y") when the claim is sourced or established. Reserve hedging syntax for claims that are genuinely speculative — and label those explicitly as speculation.

**Do this:**

> "Spiritism is deeply rooted in the indigenous people of Mexico."

> "Inspector Gómez asked Hector to recall as many names of the cultists as possible and wrote them down."

> "12 people were ultimately charged and found guilty of murder."

**Not this (generic documentary narration):**

> "Spiritism is thought to be deeply rooted in Mexican culture."

> "Gómez is believed to have written down the names Hector provided."

> "12 people may have been found guilty, though records are incomplete."

---

### Rule 2: Banned Vocabulary — No Intensifiers, Superlatives, or Clickbait Language

**Definition:** Do not use intensifiers (horrifying, shocking, disturbing, terrifying, unbelievable, chilling, harrowing, mind-blowing, incredible [used as pure emphasis]), superlatives applied emotionally (the most evil, the darkest, the worst crime ever), or clickbait escalators (you won't believe, nobody talks about this, this will change everything). When something is disturbing on its own merits, state it plainly. The word "incredibly" is used once in the reference script — as a genuine marker of factual surprise, directly followed by sourcing ("from the testimony of those who survived"). This is the only acceptable use.

**Banned words and phrases:**
horrifying, shocking, disturbing (as editorial, not as label), terrifying, unbelievable, mind-blowing, chilling, harrowing, jaw-dropping, gut-wrenching, you won't believe, nobody talks about this, hidden history (as a rhetorical hook), the darkest, the most evil.

**Do this:**

> "The Hernandez brothers were cruel and enjoyed exerting their newly found authority over the non-believers."

> "Santos and Cayetano sliced open the body and removed the victim's still-beating heart."

> "Selena picked up a stone and gave it to him, begging him to use it to crush out her life."

**Not this (generic documentary narration):**

> "What happened next is absolutely shocking."

> "In a horrifying act that defies comprehension, the brothers did the unthinkable."

> "This is one of the most disturbing cases you'll ever hear."

---

### Rule 3: Narration Scope — No Host Commentary, No Fourth-Wall Breaks

**Definition:** The narrator is invisible. Do not address the audience directly (no "you", no "we'll look at", no "stay tuned"). Do not editorialize from outside the story ("this tells us something about human nature", "what this really means is"). Do not use "smash that like button" or any platform mechanics. The only permitted exception: brief, dry editorial sentences when correcting a factual error in the public record — and even then, only once, and only when necessary. The reference script uses this once: "I mean — that's some absolutely insane homophobic nonsense." This is a hard exception, not a template.

**Do this:**

> "The twins' ritual involved the theatrical brewing of a potion which they claimed had the supernatural power to grant visions, but in reality it was laced with marijuana."

> "Gómez wasn't fooled so easily."

**Not this (generic documentary narration):**

> "Now, you might be wondering how anyone could believe this — but as we'll see, the power of belief is stronger than we think."

> "Stay with us, because what happens next changes everything."

> "This is where it gets really interesting."

---

### Rule 4: Source Attribution Syntax — Label Speculation Explicitly

**Definition:** Distinguish sourced claims, eyewitness testimony, and inferred/speculative claims using explicit syntactic markers. Do not let speculation travel without a label. Sourced claims use direct attribution ("from the testimony of those who survived", "Inspector Gómez relayed to Bill Starr"). Inferred claims use explicit inference markers ("this seems very unlikely, as…"). Speculation is not used in this channel — if a claim cannot be sourced or reasonably inferred, it is omitted or flagged.

**Sourced claim pattern:**
> "From the testimony of those who had survived the sacrifices and bloodshed yet to come, one of the villagers offered themselves willingly at the very first sacrifice."

**Inference / unlikely claim pattern:**
> "This seems very unlikely, as Monterrey is over 148 km away from Yaba Bua and across state borders."

**Source credibility ranking pattern:**
> "Whilst it's difficult to say for sure whether Bill Starr's interview of Gómez is the absolute truth of the matter, after months of research I can say it's the most trustworthy source existing on the internet."

**Not this (generic documentary narration):**
> "It's thought that the cult may have had influence beyond the village, though this is unconfirmed."

> "Experts believe the twins manipulated the farmers using psychological techniques."

---

### Rule 5: Sentence Rhythm — Short Declarative Beats After Heavy Information

**Definition:** After delivering a dense, heavy, or morally significant piece of information, drop to a short declarative sentence of under 10 words. Do not absorb short beats into longer surrounding sentences. The short beat is structural — it functions as a breath, letting the information land before the narrative continues. Do NOT add conjunctions to merge these beats with adjacent sentences.

**Do this:**

> "By the time Hector had come to his senses, it was too late. Cayetano and Santos had dragged Selena's body atop the altar and removed her heart."

> "They struggled. She fell. The others rushed in on them, knocking Hector out and pelting Selena with stones until she was senseless."

> "Selena was well and truly dead."

> "Gómez wasn't fooled so easily."

**Not this (generic documentary narration):**

> "By the time Hector had regained consciousness, Cayetano and Santos had already dragged Selena's body to the altar and removed her heart in a gruesome act."

> "They struggled, and she fell, with the others then rushing in to knock Hector out."

---

### Sentence Rhythm Patterns

**Short/long alternation observed in the clean script:**

The reference script oscillates between dense expository paragraphs (4–6 sentences of medium-to-long length, delivering historical context or narrative setup) and short declarative beats (1–3 words to one short sentence) that follow a revelation or emotional turning point.

**Pattern: Setup paragraph → revelation sentence → short beat:**

> "Selena Silvana volunteered herself. However, just before she reached the altar — finally, after months of silence — someone spoke out."

The phrase "someone spoke out" is the beat. It arrives after a long compound sentence and lands without elaboration.

**Pattern: Compressed action sequence (one sentence per action):**

> "They struggled. She fell. The others rushed in on them, knocking Hector out and pelting Selena with stones until she was senseless."

Three short sentences in succession, each one an action. The rhythm refuses to linger. The third sentence extends as the violence compounds — longer because more is happening simultaneously.

**Pattern: Paragraph cadence — begin with time/place anchor, end with implication:**

> "In the spring of 1962, the twins ordained two of the most faithful villagers to help them perform rituals and to act as muscle to extract tributes from the non-believers."

Chapters open with time or circumstance. They close with an implication that sets up the next beat — not with a summary of what just happened, but with what it means is coming.

**Pattern: Emotional beat shift — acceleration, then full stop:**

> "The Solis twins had lost control of the lies they told. They were soon to owe the lives of at least 15 people."

Long statement, then a short statement. The full stop lands harder because the surrounding rhythm has been moving.

## Narrative Arc Templates

Every template below is labeled by topic type. Do not apply a cult arc to an unrelated topic.

---

### Template A: Cult / Group Radicalization Arc

**Applicability:** `[Cult / Group Radicalization]` — Use when the story involves deliberate psychological manipulation of a community, escalating control, and an eventual rupture with outside authority. Not applicable to: unsolved disappearances, institutional corruption, internet crime.

**Chapter count:** 5–7 numbered chapters

**Act progression:**

| Act | Function |
|-----|----------|
| Hook / Intro | Quote from authority figure, compressed story overview, scale statement, misinformation note, closing formula |
| Chapter 1 | Establish the setting — community, isolation, why outsiders could gain entry |
| Chapter 2 | Initial control — how manipulation began, what technique was used, early escalation |
| Chapter 3 | Radicalization peak — the crossing of the irreversible threshold (first killing, first violation of baseline norms) |
| Chapter 4 | Rupture — the moment of intervention, the confrontation |
| Chapter 5 | Aftermath + coda — legal outcome, then pivot to the public record correction |

**Chapter-to-chapter connection patterns:**

- Chapters end with an implication or unresolved tension, not a summary. The next chapter begins by resolving or deepening that tension.
- Example: Chapter 2 ends "This conflict between the priests and their teenage leaders would come to a head at the end of April." Chapter 3 begins by dropping directly into that moment: "1962. As night fell upon Yaba Bua…"
- Time jumps between chapters are marked by a chapter heading with a date or year, not by a transitional sentence.

**Hook pattern (extracted from reference — reusable but flexible):**

1. **Opening quote** — Direct speech from an authority figure or witness. No quotation marks in the original; the voice is unmistakable. No attribution line beneath it.
   > "It was the worst thing I've had to deal with in all my years."

2. **Compressed story overview** — One or two sentences: location, year, what began, what it became. No adjectives of scale. "Snowball" is used metaphorically but precisely — it describes the mechanism, not the horror.
   > "In 1961, outsiders would arrive in the Mexican village of Yaba Bua, and over the next two years, the lies these people told would snowball into sacrifices to forgotten gods and the eventual massacre of the village by the authorities."

3. **Misinformation note** — One sentence stating that the internet has the story wrong. Only used when there is a demonstrable false narrative. Not used as a hook trick — used as a research commitment.
   > "The internet has been telling the story incorrectly since the turn of the century."

4. **Closing formula:**
   > "This is the true story of [subject] and [what drove the story]."

**Chapter title register:**

| DO: Evocative (names what the chapter feels like) | DON'T: Descriptive (summarizes events) |
|----|----|
| "Strangers in the Jungle" | "Two Outsiders Arrive at the Village" |
| "Willing Sacrifices" | "The First Human Sacrifice" |
| "Initial Control" | "How the Twins Gained Power" |
| "Truth: May 31st, 1963" | "The Police Raid and Arrest of the Twins" |

Note: "Truth" as a chapter title is used here to signal a pivot to factual reckoning — it works because it carries ironic weight against what came before.

**Truth-Seeking Coda** `[Optional — when widespread misinformation exists about the subject]`

A final chapter confronting the dominant false narrative in the public record. Structure:
- State where you started (what the internet/Wikipedia says)
- Trace the source chain backwards to its origin (what sources exist, what they cite, when they appeared)
- Identify the oldest reliable primary source
- Quote the incorrect version directly, then close with a dry editorial verdict

> "I mean — that's some absolutely insane homophobic nonsense. Until next time."

The coda closes without resolution. It does not reclaim hope or "set the record straight" in an uplifting sense — it simply establishes what is known and leaves the rest on the audience.

## Transition Phrase Library

Verbatim phrases extracted from the clean reference script. Only phrases that carry the channel's specific rhythm or framing are included. Generic connectors (furthermore, notably, however, in addition) are excluded.

---

### Temporal

Phrases that advance time within a chapter or between events:

- "In the spring of 1962…" — ground a new development in a specific season without a transitional bridge; works because it implies time has passed without stating "months later"
- "At dawn, Hector left Yaba Bua…" — time-of-day anchor used to mark the start of a consequential action, creates forward momentum
- "One year later, in 1964…" — clean year-jump with no padding; used after a section of analysis to re-enter the narrative timeline
- "During this time, at the end of each month…" — compresses a recurring pattern into a single phrase; avoids narrating each instance individually

---

### Causal

Phrases that link an action to its consequence:

- "Emboldened by the success of their scam, they traveled into…" — consequence-first construction; the cause is in the subordinate clause, the action leads the sentence
- "This proved to be a powerful and effective technique." — short declarative close after a causal explanation; delivers verdict without elaboration
- "So powerful were the drugs and lies of the Solis twins that…" — inverted causal structure; puts the magnitude before the action, slows the reader before the revelation
- "But not before discovering that the authorities were on their way." — trailing causal clause that reverses an apparent outcome; creates dread by delaying the implication

---

### Contrast / Revelation

Phrases that flip expectation or introduce a fact that contradicts what was established:

- "But Gómez wasn't fooled so easily." — short, dry reversal; arrives immediately after the character's deception claim, no setup needed
- "However, just before she reached the altar — finally, after months of silence — someone spoke out." — the dash-bounded interruption creates tension; "finally" signals that something long-anticipated has arrived
- "The internet has been telling the story incorrectly since the turn of the century." — direct factual contrast; states that established common knowledge is wrong without editorial inflation
- "I think you can see where these stories depart from each other now." — the only permitted near-host-commentary usage; used once, briefly, to mark an obvious contrast before resuming analysis

---

### Escalation

Phrases that signal the stakes are rising:

- "Each time upping the stakes." — fragments used for escalation; the short sentence after a description of the escalation pattern
- "The twins couldn't be seen as limiting the growth of the cult, especially since the brothers kept urging them towards ever greater and greater spectacles." — repetition of "greater" signals the compounding nature of escalation
- "The Solis twins had lost control of the lies they told. They were soon to owe the lives of at least 15 people." — two-sentence escalation close; the second sentence expands the scale backward to encompass what has already happened

---

### Evidential

Phrases that introduce sourcing, testimony, or a cited claim:

- "From the testimony of those who had survived the sacrifices and bloodshed yet to come…" — sourcing with temporal complexity; the phrase "yet to come" marks the testimony as retrospective, grounding the claim in real witness accounts
- "Whilst it's difficult to say for sure whether… after months of research I can say it's the most trustworthy source existing on the internet." — credibility concession followed by a reasoned verdict; honest about uncertainty, then commits to a position
- "The absolute earliest source I could find was a newspaper clipping from Texas…" — search provenance language; documents the research process rather than presenting conclusions without method
- "Looking at the edit history of the Wikipedia page, we find that…" — traceable sourcing; names the specific mechanism of investigation

## Open Ending Template

**Trigger condition:** Use when the case is unsolved, the resolution is contested, the historical record is permanently incomplete, or when what happened is known but why remains unresolvable. Do not use for cases where a clear factual resolution exists — even if that resolution is grim. The trigger is: the audience cannot know the full truth, and that not-knowing carries moral or existential weight.

**Three-part structure:**

**Part 1 — Present the final known evidence**
State what is factually established as of the script's present: who was arrested, what was recovered, what the record shows. No editorializing. No silver linings. State it plainly.

**Part 2 — Acknowledge the unknowns**
Name what is missing, disputed, or unresolvable. Do not soften. Do not say "we may never know" as a consolation — say it as a fact with specific content attached ("we don't know whether X, and the only people who did are dead").

**Part 3 — Leave weight with the audience**
One or two sentences. Do not resolve the moral question. Do not tell the audience what to feel. Do not end with: "their stories remind us", "but the victims deserve better", or any equivalent that frames tragedy as lesson. The weight must land without relief.

---

**Crafted example (channel voice):**

> 12 people were charged and convicted. The remains of 13 victims were recovered from the cave. Magdalena and Eleazar Solis were held for further investigation.
>
> What Yaba Bua looked like after the federal troops left — whether the village survived, whether the survivors remained — the record does not say. The names of most of the victims do not appear in any source.
>
> The Hernandez brothers are dead. So is Hector Solis. So is Selena Silvana. The rest of what happened in that cave over the course of a year belongs to people who either cannot or will not speak.

---

**Anti-pattern — do not use:**

> "Though we may never know the full truth, the victims' stories remind us of the resilience of the human spirit and the importance of speaking out."

> "While justice was served in part, the deeper questions this case raises about faith, manipulation, and community remain with us as a sobering reminder."

Do NOT provide artificial resolution, consolation, or a "silver lining" ending. Do NOT close with a lesson-framing that reframes tragedy as growth or meaning. The weight of not knowing must land without relief.

## Hook Patterns

The opening formula extracted from the reference script is distinctive enough to merit a standalone section.

**Annotated hook structure:**

```
[QUOTE] — Raw quote from an authority figure or witness. No quotation marks in the original;
          the voice is unmistakable. No attribution line beneath it.

[COMPRESSED OVERVIEW] — One or two sentences: location, year, what began, what it became.
                         No adjectives of scale. "Snowball" is used metaphorically but
                         precisely — it describes the mechanism, not the horror.

[MISINFORMATION FLAG] — One sentence stating that the internet has the story wrong.
                         Only used when there is a demonstrable false narrative.
                         Not used as a hook trick — used as a research commitment.

[CLOSING FORMULA] — "This is the true story of [X] and [what drove it]."
                     Always "true story." Always the subject, then the driving force.
```

**Why this hook works:** The opening quote borrows authority (it's from a police inspector, not a narrator). The compressed overview delivers the entire story in two sentences — the audience already knows the destination, and the video becomes about understanding how it got there. The misinformation flag signals: "we did original research." The closing formula is not a teaser — it's a commitment.

## Chapter Naming Register

**Rule:** Chapter titles name what the chapter *feels like* — the emotional or moral register — not what happens in it. A chapter about someone gaining trust over time is called "Initial Control," not "How the Twins Consolidated Power." A chapter about a moment of confrontation and death is called "Truth: May 31st, 1963," not "The Final Sacrifice and Police Raid."

**The "Truth:" prefix pattern:**

This prefix is used for chapters that pivot from narrative to reckoning. "Truth: May 31st, 1963" and "Truth: 2024" both use this prefix — one for the factual rupture inside the story, one for the factual reckoning with how the story has been told. The prefix works because it carries ironic weight: for most of the video, the audience has been watching lies. "Truth" arriving as a chapter heading signals the reversal.

**Full contrast table:**

| Evocative (channel style) | Descriptive (avoid) |
|---|---|
| Strangers in the Jungle | Two Outsiders Arrive |
| Initial Control | The Twins Gain Power |
| Willing Sacrifices | The First Human Sacrifice Takes Place |
| Truth: May 31st, 1963 | The Police Raid |
| Truth: 2024 | Corrections to the Online Narrative |

**When to use numbers vs titles alone:**

The reference script numbers all chapters (1. Strangers in the Jungle). Numbering is standard. The title is mandatory. The number helps the audience track position in a long video; the title sets expectation.
